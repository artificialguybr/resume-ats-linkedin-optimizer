# Cover Letters

A cover letter rarely gets you hired on its own, but a weak or generic one gets
you cut — and a sharp, specific one tips close calls. Write one when the posting
asks for it, when there's a referral/context worth stating, or when you're a
career-changer who needs to connect the dots. Skip the generic template blast.

## When to include one
- The application asks for it (always comply).
- You have a genuine hook: a referral, a specific reason for this company, or a
  gap/pivot the resume can't explain on its own.
- Skip it (or keep it one tight paragraph) for high-volume portals that treat it
  as optional and you have nothing specific to add.

## Structure (4 short paragraphs, ≤ 1 page, ~250–350 words)

1. **Hook (2–3 sentences).** Name the role and company, and open with the single
   most relevant, quantified reason you're a strong fit — not "I am writing to
   apply for…". Reference something specific about the company if you can.
2. **Proof (1–2 paragraphs).** Pick your **2–3 most relevant** achievements from
   the resume and expand them with context the resume couldn't hold — the "why it
   mattered," the situation, the outcome. Mirror the job's language. Don't
   restate the whole resume.
3. **Fit / motivation (short).** Why *this* company and role specifically — show
   you researched it. Connect your trajectory to what they need. For
   career-changers, this is where you bridge the gap honestly.
4. **Close + CTA.** Reaffirm interest, thank them, and invite the next step.

## Rules
- **Address a person** if you can find the hiring manager; else "Dear [Team]
  Hiring Team." Avoid "To Whom It May Concern."
- **Tailor every letter.** A generic letter is worse than none — recruiters spot
  it instantly.
- **Don't repeat the resume verbatim** — add context and voice.
- **Quantify** the same way as the resume; lead with impact.
- **Same zero-fabrication rule** — everything must be true.
- **Run the de-slop pass** — cover letters attract buzzwords worst of all. No
  "I am a results-driven, passionate team player." Show, don't claim.
- **Match the format** to the resume header (same name/contact block) for a
  consistent package.
- **One page, always.** Tighter is better; busy readers skim.

## Common mistakes
- Generic / not tailored. · Restating the resume. · Opening with "I am writing to
  apply…". · All about what *you* want, nothing about their needs. · Too long. ·
  Buzzword soup. · Typos (fatal — same as the resume). · Wrong company name left
  in from a reused template (instant reject).

## Quick template

> Dear [Hiring Manager name / Team],
>
> [Hook: role + company + your single strongest, quantified, relevant fit.]
>
> [Proof: 1–2 achievements expanded with context and outcome, mirroring the JD.]
>
> [Fit: a specific, researched reason this company/role — and how your path maps
> to what they need.]
>
> [Close: reaffirm interest + thank + invite next step.]
>
> Sincerely,
> [Name]
