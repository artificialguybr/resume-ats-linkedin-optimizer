# Content & Bullet Writing (Human Readability)

What makes a resume actually win interviews once it's parsed. This is where most
resumes are won or lost, because a human decides in ~7 seconds.

## The bullet formula (everyone agrees on this)

**Action verb + what you did + quantified impact + (how / context)**

Equivalent framings:
- **Google / XYZ (Laszlo Bock):** "Accomplished **[X]**, as measured by **[Y]**,
  by doing **[Z]**." → *"Increased sales 25% by launching a new product line in Q1."*
- **CAR (Challenge–Action–Result)** — the resume-length compression. (STAR is the
  longer interview-story version.)

Rules:
- Start **every** bullet with a strong verb — never a noun, never a date.
- **Lead with the result, not the task.** "Cut response time 40%" ≫ "Was
  responsible for responding to tickets."
- One line per bullet where possible; front-load impact.
- Put your strongest, most-quantified, most-relevant bullets **first**. The top
  1–3 bullets of the most recent role get the most attention.
- Most recent role: ~5–7 bullets. Older roles: ~2–3.

## Weak words to eliminate (recruiters filter on these)

`Responsible for`, `Duties included`, `Helped`, `Assisted with`, `Worked on`,
`Handled`, `Tasked with`, `In charge of`, `Participated in`, `Familiar with`.
Also: personal pronouns (I/we/my), passive voice, narrative style, slang.

## Verbs: prefer plain and strong

Use concrete, plain verbs: **led, built, designed, cut, shipped, grew, launched,
reduced, increased, automated, negotiated, delivered.** Full categorized bank:
`assets/action-verbs.md`.

**Important nuance (resolve the contradiction):** buzzword-inflated verbs like
*spearheaded, orchestrated, architected, leveraged, utilized, championed,
pioneered* read as AI-generated slop to many modern recruiters. Prefer the plain
equivalent (led, coordinated, designed, used, advocated for, introduced). Don't
ship a "power verb" that trips the slop filter. See `assets/ai-slop-blacklist.md`.

## Quantification (including when you "don't have numbers")

Quantify across more than money. Metric types that impress:
- **Money:** revenue, cost saved, budget managed.
- **Percentage / change:** % up/down, before→after ("3.2 → 4.7/5", "3 days → 8 hrs").
- **Scope / volume:** people/clients/users served, team size, # projects, volume.
- **Time / speed:** time saved, faster turnaround, deadlines beaten.
- **Quality:** error reduction, satisfaction/NPS, rankings, awards.

When exact numbers are missing (never fabricate — see prime directive):
1. **Ranges/estimates** are legitimate and expected: "Supervised 7–12 students/yr."
2. **Multiply frequency:** small per-instance saving × how often = a real number.
3. **Quantify inputs** if outputs are unknown: team size, budget, accounts, tickets/day.
4. Ask **"what changed because of my work?"** then attach a number, %, timeframe,
   or volume — always confirmed by the user, and hedged with "~/approximately".
   Benchmark ranges to prompt estimates: `assets/metric-proxies.md`.

## The domain "secret weapon"

Add domain/context to generic work — cheap, huge signal gain:
- "Developed web apps" → "Developed **fintech credit-management and
  card-issuance** web platforms."
- "Managed a team" → "Managed a **6-person data team supporting a $40M
  e-commerce P&L**."

## Summary vs Objective

- **Professional Summary** (backward-looking; results and value) — default if
  ≥1–2 years relevant experience. 3–5 lines. Template:
  > [Title/identity] with [X years] in [domain], specializing in [2–3 skills].
  > Delivered [flagship quantified achievement]. Seeking to [value for target role].
- **Objective** (forward-looking; goals) — only for new grads / entry-level /
  career-changers bridging a gap. 1–2 lines. Example:
  > "Former educator with 5 years of curriculum-design experience transitioning
  > into instructional design, bringing proven skills in [transferable X, Y]."

The top section matters because a vague/missing one forces recruiters to hunt for
context — a top rejection driver.

## Recruiter skim behavior (design for it)

- Decision in ~**7.4 seconds** (Ladders eye-tracking); ~80% of that on **name,
  current title & company, previous title & company, dates, education.**
- Reading is an **F-pattern**, not top-to-bottom: current role → previous role →
  dates (checking progression) → education.
- Keywords in the **first 3 bullets** get maximum visibility. Left-aligned dates
  process ~13% faster than right-aligned.
- Therefore: strongest credentials at the top; title/company/dates instantly
  scannable; best quantified wins in the first three bullets of the recent role.

## Red flags and how to handle them (honestly)

- **Employment gaps:** be transparent, frame positively (caregiving, education,
  freelance, health, layoff). A short "Career Break" entry or a hybrid layout
  works. Explaining a gap beats hiding it.
- **Job hopping:** consolidate similar short stints under one heading (e.g.,
  "Freelance Designer, 2021–2023") and show progression/impact. A pattern is a
  flag; one or two short roles are not.

## Length, tense, person, voice

- **Length:** see `01-ats-and-formatting.md` (1 page <10 yrs; 2 pages beyond).
- **Person:** no pronouns; implied first person.
- **Tense:** past tense for past roles; present for the current role; be consistent.
- **Voice:** active, never passive.

## Common human-rejection reasons

1. Typos/grammar (the #1 dealbreaker). 2. Not tailored. 3. Weak/missing summary.
4. Duty-based bullets with no metrics. 5. Missing JD keywords. 6. Over-designed
multi-column layouts. 7. Bad/incomplete contact info. 8. Irrelevant padding.

## Before → after (the rewrite recipe in action)

Recipe: strip "responsible for" → pick a specific plain verb → state what changed
with a number → add before/after or scope → append the "how."

| Before (weak) | After (strong) |
|---|---|
| Responsible for managing social media accounts. | Grew Instagram following from 2,400 to 11,000 in 9 months by launching a weekly takeover series, lifting event attendance 35%. |
| Helped organize fundraising events. | Coordinated 4 fundraising events of 200+ attendees each, raising $24,000 — 60% above the prior year. |
| Responsible for customer service. | Cut escalation response time 40% and raised CSAT from 3.2 to 4.7/5 by building a ticket-prioritization system. |
| Managed inventory. | Reduced stock-outs 30% and excess inventory 20% by implementing automated tracking and streamlining procurement. |
| Increased sales. | Increased sales 25% by launching a new product line in Q1. |
